Executive Summary

Healthcare insurance claims processing is one of the most document-intensive, regulation-sensitive, and time-critical workflows in the insurance industry. A single hospitalization claim can involve discharge summaries, itemized hospital bills, doctor prescriptions, diagnostic reports, pre-authorization letters, and policy certificates — all arriving in different formats, from different providers, at different times.

This project automates 80% of the healthcare claims pipeline using eight specialized AI agents built on Microsoft Azure, Copilot Studio, and a Python FastAPI backend. Human reviewers remain in control of uncertain, high-value, and fraud-suspected cases.

Problem Statement in Layman Terms

Current State Pain Points in Healthcare Claims

Healthcare insurance claims today follow this broken path:

  • Fragmented intake: Patients submit claims through hospital counters, email, fax, or web portals — no unified channel — processing starts late and inconsistently.
  • Incomplete documentation: Hospital bills arrive without itemization, discharge summaries are missing, prescriptions are handwritten and illegible — manual staff chase hospitals and patients for 7–21 days.
  • Manual policy verification: Staff manually cross-check whether the diagnosis is covered, whether the hospital is in-network, whether the pre-authorization was obtained — error-prone and slow.
  • Document chaos: PDFs, scanned images, handwritten forms, DICOM reports — no structured extraction — staff re-read the same document multiple times across departments.
  • Fraud blind spots: Duplicate billing, inflated procedure codes, ghost patients — only high-value claims get manual review — patterns are missed and fraud costs grow.
  • Human bottleneck: A single medical review team handles all uncertain claims — decisions take days — patients and hospitals wait without visibility.
  • Regulatory complexity: HIPAA compliance, pre-authorization requirements, network restrictions, and benefit limits add layers of manual checking that slow every claim.
Real-world analogy: Imagine a hospital ER where every patient arrives with scattered papers in different languages. Nurses manually check if papers are complete, re-read them at each station, and a single doctor reviews all uncertain cases. No wonder the process is slow, inconsistent, and error-prone.
Your Solution

A smart healthcare claims triage system with eight specialized AI agents that collect, validate, extract, score, and route claims automatically — but human medical reviewers stay in control of the final decision for complex and uncertain cases.

Solution Vision

What You Are Building

A healthcare claims processing factory with eight specialized AI agents, each responsible for one stage of the claim lifecycle:

  • Intake Agent: Collects patient ID, policy ID, hospital name, admission/discharge dates, diagnosis, and claim amount from chat, email, or web portal.
  • Validation Agent: Verifies patient identity, confirms policy is active, checks hospital network status, and validates pre-authorization if required.
  • Missing Info Agent: Identifies missing required documents (e.g., discharge summary, itemized bill) and prompts the patient or hospital to upload them.
  • Document Agent: Extracts structured data from hospital bills, discharge summaries, prescriptions, and diagnostic reports using Azure Document Intelligence.
  • Risk Agent: Flags suspicious patterns — duplicate billing, inflated amounts, out-of-network providers, diagnosis-procedure mismatches, and known fraud indicators.
  • Adjudication Agent: Applies healthcare policy rules, coverage limits, deductibles, co-pay calculations, and network restrictions to recommend approve/reject/review.
  • Human Review Agent: Routes uncertain or high-risk claims to a medical reviewer in Microsoft Teams with full context, AI recommendation, and one-click approve/reject.
  • CSR Agent: Answers patient and hospital queries about claim status, provides transparency on decisions, and communicates next steps.

Key Design Principles

⚡ Speed

Straightforward claims with complete documentation auto-approve in under 60 seconds. No human waits for routine cases.

🎛️ Control

Uncertain, high-value, and fraud-suspected cases route to human medical reviewers with full context and AI recommendation.

🔍 Transparency

Every decision records rationale, confidence score, policy clause reference, and audit trail. Patients see status anytime.

🔒 Compliance

HIPAA-aligned data handling, encrypted document storage, role-based access, and complete audit logs for every action.

🤱

Covers normal delivery, C-section, pre/post-natal care, and newborn coverage for the first 90 days. Subject to 9-month waiting period and maternity sub-limit.
💊

Lump-sum benefit paid upon first diagnosis of a covered critical illness (cancer, heart attack, stroke, kidney failure, major organ transplant). Requires specialist confirmation and survival period of 30 days post-diagnosis.

System Architecture

High-Level Architecture

The architecture separates patient-facing intake from claim orchestration and medical review. Claims flow through the backend for validation, scoring, and persistence while Azure services provide document extraction and policy search.

Architecture Principles

  • Event-driven: Claims progress through stages via events and state changes recorded in Dataverse.
  • Stateless: Backend services are stateless; all claim state lives in Dataverse.
  • Modular: Each agent is independent and can be updated without affecting others.
  • Observable: Every action logs events for audit, debugging, and HIPAA compliance.
  • Scalable: Azure services auto-scale based on claim volume.
  • HIPAA-aligned: PHI is encrypted at rest and in transit; access is role-based.

  2. Intake Agent — Sends claim to POST /api/claims/intake and returns claim_id: CLM-hlt-abc123.
  3. Validation Agent — Checks policy in Dataverse: active, hospitalization covered, hospital is in-network, pre-authorization obtained.
  4. Missing Info Agent — Detects that the itemized bill is missing. Asks Priya to upload it. Records the request.
  5. Document Agent — Extracts admission_date, discharge_date, diagnosis_code (ICD-10: J18.9), total_amount (RM 8,200), and hospital_name from uploaded PDFs.

Decision Thresholds Explained

✅ Confidence ≥ 0.75 & Risk Score < 0.3

Status: APPROVED
Action: Auto-approve immediately, calculate payout (sum insured − deductible − co-pay), notify patient, update Dataverse.

⏳ Confidence 0.45–0.75 or Risk Score 0.3–0.6

Status: PENDING_HUMAN_REVIEW
Action: Escalate to Teams medical review channel with full context, AI recommendation, and one-click approve/reject card.

❌ Confidence < 0.45 or Risk Score > 0.6

Status: REJECTED or ESCALATED (Priority)
Action: Auto-reject with reason, or escalate with high-priority flag to fraud investigation team.

Azure Blob Storage — Organized Document Architecture

The platform uses two separate Azure Blob Storage containers within one single storage account. One container holds hospital policy PDFs (admin-managed), the other holds patient claim documents (patient-uploaded). Both containers share a single connection string — the backend code decides which container to use based on the type of file being stored.

✅ Answer: One Connection String, Two Containers

Yes — a single AZURE_STORAGE_CONNECTION_STRING gives access to the entire storage account. The backend uses two separate container names (hospital-policy-documents and patient-claim-documents) to keep the files logically separated. You do not need two storage accounts or two connection strings. One account, one key, two containers.

How the Connection String Works

The connection string is a single string that contains the storage account name, account key, and endpoint. It looks like this:

DefaultEndpointsProtocol=https;AccountName=sth2fhealthdev;AccountKey=YOUR_KEY_HERE;EndpointSuffix=core.windows.net

In the backend code (app.py), this one string is used to create a BlobServiceClient, which can then access any container in that account:

# One client, two containers — both accessed via the same connection string
from azure.storage.blob import BlobServiceClient

client = BlobServiceClient.from_connection_string(os.getenv("AZURE_STORAGE_CONNECTION_STRING"))

# Access Container 1 — hospital policies
policy_container = client.get_container_client("hospital-policy-documents")

# Access Container 2 — patient claim documents
claims_container = client.get_container_client("patient-claim-documents")

Your .env File — Complete Blob Storage Section

# ── Azure Blob Storage ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# ONE connection string gives access to BOTH containers below
AZURE_STORAGE_CONNECTION_STRING=DefaultEndpointsProtocol=https;AccountName=sth2fhealthdev;AccountKey=YOUR_KEY_HERE;EndpointSuffix=core.windows.net

How to Call the Backend APIs — Complete Beginner Guide

Every agent in Copilot Studio needs to call the backend API to do its work. Here is exactly how to do it, step by step, for each endpoint.

What is an API Call?

An API call is like sending a message to the backend and getting a reply. You send data (the "request") and receive data back (the "response"). In Copilot Studio, you do this using an Action node that calls an HTTP endpoint.

Step-by-Step: How to Add an API Call in Copilot Studio

  1. In your topic editor, click + Add node at the point where you want to call the API.
  2. Select "Call an action" → then "Create a flow" (this opens Power Automate).
  3. In Power Automate, add an HTTP action: Method = POST or GET, URI = your backend URL, Headers = Content-Type: application/json and Authorization: Bearer {token}.
  4. In the Body field, paste the JSON payload with your bot variables substituted in (e.g., @{variables('patient_id')}).
  5. Add a Parse JSON action after the HTTP call to extract the response fields into variables.
  6. Return the variables back to Copilot Studio as output parameters.

Backend Architecture Deep Dive

The backend uses Flask/FastAPI to expose healthcare claim and admin APIs. The orchestrator handles all claim stage transitions and calls Azure services for medical document extraction and AI adjudication.

Service Responsibilities

  • Intake Service: Creates claim record, assigns claim_id, records intake event.
  • Validation Service: Checks patient, policy, network status, pre-auth, waiting period.
  • Document Service: Calls Azure Document Intelligence, stores extracted fields, records extraction event.
  • Risk Service: Runs fraud detection rules, calculates risk_score, records risk_flagged event.
  • Adjudication Service: Calls Azure OpenAI with policy context, calculates confidence and approved_amount, records adjudicated event.
  • Human Review Service: Triggers Power Automate flow, waits for Teams response, records human_approved/rejected event.

Healthcare Adjudication Formula

# Confidence Score Calculation
coverage_score = 1.0 if claim_type in policy.covered_types else 0.0
evidence_score = len(uploaded_docs) / len(required_docs)  # 0.0 to 1.0
risk_penalty = risk_score  # 0.0 to 1.0

confidence = (0.45 * coverage_score) + (0.40 * evidence_score) - (0.35 * risk_penalty)

# Payout Calculation
eligible_amount = min(claim_amount, policy.coverage_limit)
approved_amount = eligible_amount - policy.deductible - (policy.co_pay_pct * eligible_amount)

Security & HIPAA Best Practices

Healthcare claims contain Protected Health Information (PHI). Every design decision must account for data privacy, access control, and audit requirements.

Data Protection
  • Encrypt PHI at rest (Azure Storage encryption) and in transit (HTTPS/TLS 1.2+).
  • Store patient IC numbers and medical data in encrypted fields in Dataverse.
  • Never log PHI in application logs — use claim_id references only.
  • Use Azure Key Vault for all secrets and connection strings.
Access Control
  • Role-based access: patients see only their own claims; reviewers see assigned claims; admins see all.
  • Protect all admin endpoints with token-based authentication.
  • Use Dataverse row-level security for claim data.
  • Medical reviewers in Teams can only act on claims assigned to them.
Audit & Compliance
  • Every state transition creates a CLAIM_EVENT record — this is your HIPAA audit trail.
  • Record actor (system/reviewer/patient), timestamp, and action for every event.
  • Retain audit logs for minimum 7 years (HIPAA requirement).
  • Log all admin actions (policy uploads, rule changes, manual approvals).
Document Security
  • Validate uploaded files: check MIME type, file size (<16MB), and scan for malware.
  • Store medical documents in private Azure Blob Storage — never public.
  • Generate time-limited SAS tokens for document access — never expose permanent URLs.
  • Delete temporary files after processing.

Responsible AI Controls

Healthcare AI decisions directly affect patient welfare. Every AI recommendation must be transparent, auditable, and human-supervised for uncertain cases.

  • Explainability: Every adjudication decision records the confidence score, coverage score, evidence score, risk penalty, and the specific policy clauses cited. Patients can request a full explanation.
  • Human oversight: Claims with confidence 0.45–0.75 always route to a human medical reviewer. No AI auto-approves borderline cases.
  • Bias monitoring: Regularly audit approval rates across patient demographics to detect and correct any systematic bias in the AI model.
  • Confidence thresholds: The 0.75 auto-approval threshold is conservative by design. Adjust only after reviewing false positive/negative rates.
  • Patient transparency: Patients receive clear status messages, rejection reasons, and next steps. No opaque "claim denied" without explanation.
  • Model grounding: Azure OpenAI responses are grounded with actual policy documents via AI Search — the model cannot hallucinate coverage that doesn't exist.
  • Override capability: Human reviewers can always override AI recommendations. Their decision is final and recorded with their identity and timestamp.
